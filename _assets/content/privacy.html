Websites, blogs, and apps provided by Brian Hodgkiss Injury Lawyers use cookies to collect certain information from visitors, even when they aren’t logged in. We may record the following information:

You can disable cookies by adjusting your browser settings. You can choose to have your computer warn you each time a cookie is being sent, or you can choose to turn off all cookies. California Online Privacy Protection Act (CalOPPA)

CalOPPA is the first state law in the nation to require commercial websites and online services to include a privacy policy. The law’s reach stretches well beyond California to require a person or company that operates websites collecting personally identifiable information from California consumers to post a conspicuous privacy policy on its website stating exactly the information being collected and the individuals with whom it is being shared, and to comply with this policy. Learn more about CalOPPA at the Consumer Federation of California’s website. In compliance with CalOPPA, we agree to the following:
  • Users can visit our site anonymously.
  • We have created a privacy policy and a link to it on our home page, or at a minimum, on the first significant page upon entering our website.
  • Our privacy policy link includes the word “privacy” and can easily be found on the page specified above.
  • Users will be notified of any privacy policy changes on our Privacy Policy page.
  • Users are able to change their personal information by calling us.

Fair Information Practices

The Fair Information Practice Principles form the backbone of privacy law in the United States. In order to be in line with Fair Information Practice Principles, we will take the following responsive action, should a data breach occur:
  • We will notify affected users via email within 7 business days.
  • We will notify affected users via phone call within 7 business days.
We also agree to the individual redress principle, which requires that individuals have a right to pursue legally enforceable rights against data collectors and processors who fail to adhere to the law. This principle requires not only that individuals have enforceable rights against data users, but also that individuals have recourse to courts or government agencies to investigate and/or prosecute non-compliance by data processors.
